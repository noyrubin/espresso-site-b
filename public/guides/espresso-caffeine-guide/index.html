How Much Caffeine in a Shot of Espresso? (The Exact Numbers)

A single shot of espresso contains 63–75 mg of caffeine. Here's how that compares to drip coffee, what changes the number, and when to cut yourself off.

A single shot of espresso (1 oz / 30 ml) contains 63–75 mg of caffeine. A double shot (doppio) contains 126–150 mg.

That’s less than most people expect — and less than a standard 8 oz drip coffee, which averages 95 mg. The confusion comes from concentration: espresso is highly concentrated, but you drink far less of it.

Note: The FDA considers up to 400 mg/day safe for healthy adults. That’s roughly 5–6 single espresso shots.

What Determines the Caffeine in a Shot?#

Several factors shift the final caffeine content:

1. Dose (How Much Coffee You Use)#

The single biggest variable. A single shot typically uses 7–9 g of ground coffee. A double shot uses 14–18 g. More coffee = more caffeine, linearly.

2. Roast Level#

Lighter roasts have slightly more caffeine than dark roasts — roasting degrades caffeine by a small amount. The difference is minor (a few milligrams per shot), but the myth that dark roast is “stronger” in caffeine is false. Dark roast is stronger in flavor, not caffeine.

3. Grind Size and Extraction Time#

A standard espresso extracts in 25–30 seconds. A longer pull (lungo) extracts more caffeine. A shorter pull (ristretto) extracts less. Extraction efficiency typically ranges from 18–22% of available caffeine.

4. Coffee Bean Variety#

Robusta beans contain roughly twice the caffeine of Arabica. Most espresso blends use Arabica-dominant beans. Some traditional Italian blends add Robusta for body and crema — these will have noticeably more caffeine.

5. Machine and Pressure#

Standard espresso machines extract at 9 bars of pressure. The pressure affects flavor and body more than caffeine content. Caffeine is water-soluble and extracts efficiently across a normal pressure range.

Caffeine in Milk-Based Espresso Drinks#

The espresso is the caffeine source in any milk drink. Adding milk doesn’t reduce caffeine — it just dilutes concentration.

At Starbucks, a tall latte uses one shot; a grande and venti use two shots. So a grande latte has about the same caffeine as a small latte — you’re paying for milk, not more caffeine.

How to Manage Your Espresso Caffeine Intake#

Timing matters more than total amount. Caffeine has a half-life of 5–7 hours. A double shot at 2 pm means ~65 mg of caffeine still circulating at 9 pm.

A practical rule: no espresso after 2 pm if you want to sleep by 10–11 pm.

Morning use: A double shot on waking (126–150 mg) is well within safe limits and effective for most adults. Some people add a second double shot mid-morning — still under 300 mg total.

Afternoon: A single shot (63–75 mg) at noon is manageable for most people without sleep impact.

Sensitive to caffeine? Start with a single ristretto shot (~50 mg). Watch for jitteriness, anxiety, or racing heart — these are signs you’re over your personal threshold.

Frequently Asked Questions#

Is espresso stronger than coffee? Per ounce, yes — espresso is 5–10x more concentrated. But a full 8 oz cup of drip coffee contains more total caffeine than a single 1 oz espresso shot. It depends on how you measure “stronger.”

Does a darker roast have more caffeine? No. Lighter roasts retain slightly more caffeine. The “stronger” flavor of dark roast is from Maillard reaction compounds, not caffeine.

How much caffeine is in a Starbucks espresso shot? Starbucks uses 75 mg per solo shot, 150 mg per doppio. Their blonde roast shots run slightly higher (~85 mg per shot) because it’s lighter roasted.

Can espresso cause caffeine overdose? Extremely rare at normal consumption. You’d need ~5,000 mg to reach dangerous toxicity — that’s 65+ double shots. The 400 mg/day guideline is for maintaining safe daily intake, not a cliff edge.

What’s the caffeine in decaf espresso? Decaf espresso is not caffeine-free. It typically contains 5–15 mg per shot, versus 63–75 mg for regular.

The Bottom Line#

One shot of espresso: 63–75 mg of caffeine. One double shot: 126–150 mg. Less than a cup of drip coffee by volume, more per ounce. Most café drinks are built on double shots and land in the 126–150 mg range regardless of drink size.

Keep daily intake under 400 mg, stop drinking espresso by early afternoon, and you’ll stay well within both safety guidelines and good sleep hygiene.
